Cave

Advanced Studio 4 "Knowledge Territories"

Columbia GSAPP

Critic: Nahyun Hwang, Partner: Cherry Qu

2020


The focus of this studio encompasses the often murky boundaries of urban and rural ecologies, termed "r/urban". These ecologies offer complex yet open interpretations to rural developments after the "Urban Age". This project re-evaluates the scars of Kingston's industrial past.

Below the city’s first urban park sits an abandoned limestone mine called the Kingston Cave. Hidden and disused, this subterranean network represents an uncomfortable dichotomy between Kingston’s past prosperity and present ennui. Co-opted at least once in its history as a home for a wonderfully strange mushroom farm, the Kingston Cave can once again be engaged by the community.

This proposal attempts to establish a communal school yard within the Kingston Cave that publicizes both its unique setting as well as its marred history. At the center of this proposal stems an interest in remediating the abuses rampant in the creation of these mines and on the exploitation of child labor during that period of extraction. Specifically, intergenerational programs such as a camp, an aeroponic farm and communal kitchen, a playground and a subterranean research lab attempts to recontextualize the human activity with this site. Besides accommodating for the arts and sciences, children are free to engage with the site in numerous ways. Through community programs that merge education, science, farming and play, the underground site attempts to foster the imaginations and creativity of the young through active learning and engagement.
